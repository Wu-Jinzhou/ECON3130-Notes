The Big Picture: Estimation
We use random variables to represent uncertain outcomes.

Examples: Next month’s change in the S&P500.
         Income of a household in the U.S.
         The percentage of households that have AT&T mobile phone service.

Problem is: Typically we don’t know the probability distribution of interest.

         E.g., We don’t know the expected value, variance, etc.,
         of next month's change in the S&P500.

What can we do about this in practice? We estimate these properties with
data, by computing statistics that are good approximations (we hope) to the
unknowns.
                                                                              4




Samples and Populations
How do we do this estimation?
We collect a sample of (hopefully) representative outcomes of the
random variable (the uncertain situation).

These samples are usually either:
  • Survey results from investigating a small representative sample of outcomes (e.g.,
    election polls, satisfaction surveys, internet query).
  • Past realizations of the random process (e.g. stock market data, past demands,
    profits, GDP, etc.)


We view the sample as a representative subset of some population
(the whole class of individuals, days, etc., we would like to
characterize).

Populations vs. Samples
• When analyzing populations:
  • Relationships are what they are
  • No uncertainty about results or statistical inference


• Samples are incomplete sets of observations from an
 underlying population

Goal: Say something about the population using the
       sample data (i.e., statistical inference)
